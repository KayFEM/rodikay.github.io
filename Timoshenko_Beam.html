Timoshenkos Beam Theory
Beams are fundamental components in many engineering structures, such as bridges, buildings, and machines. The
accurate analysis of beam behavior under various loads and conditions is critical for ensuring the safety and stability
of these structures. Over the years, various theories have been developed to describe the behavior of beams, with
Timoshenkos beam theory being one of the most widely used and rened approaches.
Timoshenkos beam theory accept the assumptions of that the deections in the transverse sections are small, and there
are zero transverse displacement. Unlike the traditional Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, that assumes that the transverse
cross section remains plane and orthogonal to the beam axis after deformation, Timoshenkos accept a new assumption:
Plane section orthogonal to the beam axis before deection remain plane but not necessarily are they orthogonal to the
beam axis after deformation.
One of the key features of Timoshenkos beam theory is the separation of the stiffness matrix into two parts: a longitu-
dinal part, which describes the stiffness of the beam due to bending, and a shear part, which describes the stiffness of
the beam due to torsion. This split of the stiffness matrix allows for a more precise analysis of the behavior of beams
under various loads.
